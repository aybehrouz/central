Consider a standard decision problem in Bayesian decision theory.
An agent must choose an action from a set of available alternatives.
The resulting loss depends on an unknown state of the environment and is quantified by a real-valued loss function.
Specifically, let $a$ denote an action and $x$ the unknown state.
The loss associated with choosing action $a$ when the state is $x$ is given by the loss function $\util$.

We assume that some evidence $e$ about the unknown state is available and can be used to guide the agent's decision.
In other words, the agent aims to find an optimal strategy, where a strategy is a conditional distribution over
actions given the observed evidence, denoted by $S(A \mid E)$.

In Bayesian decision theory, the optimal strategy is the one that minimizes the expected loss:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{\text{opt}} = \arg\min_S\sum_a\sum_{x,e} \util S(a \mid e)\envDist(x, e).
\end{equation*}

One can show that there always exists a deterministic optimal strategy,
namely a strategy that, for each evidence $e$, selects the action minimizing the expected loss conditioned
on $e$:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{\text{opt}}(e) = \arg\min_a \Ex{\envDist}{\util \mid e}.
\end{equation*}

Now suppose that, instead of basing our decisions on the original evidence $E$,
we choose to use a transformed version~$E'$.
In practice, the evidence available to the agent is fixed by the environment.
However, the agent may choose to base their decisions on a deterministic transformation $E' = f(E)$.
This is analogous to feature selection in machine learning, where a high-dimensional observation is replaced by a
lower-dimensional representation.

Could such a transformation improve the agent's strategy?
Within the framework of Bayesian decision theory, this question has a straightforward answer.
Any two strategies can be compared through their expected loss: the strategy with the lower expected loss is preferred.
However, a fundamental result of Bayesian decision theory states that the optimal strategy based on $E$ is always at
least as good as any strategy based on~$E'$.

One might argue that adopting a strategy that uses simpler evidence may discard information and is therefore not
justified by Bayesian decision theory.
The key point here is that the space of strategies using~$E'$ is a subset of the space of strategies using~$E$.
When we search for the optimal strategy based on $E$, we are simultaneously considering all strategies that
depend on $E'$, since every such strategy can also be expressed as a strategy based on $E$.
This helps explain why standard Bayesian inference does not inherently favor simpler representations of the evidence.

Although the optimal Bayesian strategy based on $E$ can always exploit all available information, the joint
distribution $\envDist(x,e)$ determines which aspects of the evidence are actually useful for predicting the unknown
state.
With an appropriate choice of $\envDist(x,e)$, the optimal strategy based on $E$ may effectively ignore irrelevant
components of the evidence and behave as though it were based on a lower-dimensional representation $E'$.
In this sense, Bayesian inference can perform a form of automatic feature selection.

In many statistical models, specifying $\envDist(x,e)$ amounts to choosing a prior distribution.
Consequently, the ability to promote relevant information depends on how prior knowledge about the environment is
encoded.
The difficulty, therefore, does not lie in Bayesian decision theory itself.
Rather, it lies in how partial knowledge is represented.
If partial knowledge is represented appropriately, standard Bayesian inference can naturally extract relevant
information while disregarding irrelevant aspects of the evidence.
Many commonly used priors, including flat priors, do not generally provide this behavior.
The central distribution framework addresses this problem by providing a principled representation of partial
knowledge for Bayesian inference.

In this paper, we introduce the central distribution, an information-theoretic representation of partial
knowledge that converts set-valued, and more generally hierarchical, uncertainty into a single probability
distribution suitable for Bayesian inference.

We establish the theoretical foundations of the framework by developing a theory of central distributions for finite
sets of probability distributions, including existence, uniqueness, and minimax characterizations.
We introduce the corresponding notion of central priors for parametric models and identify a sufficient fixed-point
condition linking central priors to Bayesian reference analysis~\cite{BERNARDO200517}.

Building on these foundations, we investigate asymptotic extensions of the framework and develop a practical
approximation method for constructing central distributions.
Finally, we illustrate how representing uncertainty about model structure through hierarchical central distributions
allows standard Bayesian inference to naturally perform feature selection.
