Suppose we want to approximate the central distribution of a hierarchy of distributions \set{h}.
To do so, we need a measure that quantifies the quality of an approximation.
We argue that a natural choice is the central divergence introduced in Definition~\ref{def:central-divergence}.
Since central distributions are intended to serve as representatives of hierarchies of distributions,
central divergence may be viewed as a measure of how well a probability distribution represents a hierarchy.

At first glance, computing this quantity appears to require explicit knowledge of the central distribution of \set{h}.
However, it turns out that the central divergence admits an upper bound that can be estimated without explicitly
knowing the central distribution.

In variational settings, it is often more convenient to work with unnormalized measures rather than normalized
distributions.
Therefore, we state the next theorem in terms of unnormalized measures.
We assume throughout that $\tilde P(x) > 0$ whenever $Q(x) > 0$
for some \inSet{Q}, so that all logarithms and KL divergences are well-defined.

\begin{theorem}\label{th:central-approx}
    Let \setQ be a finite set of probability distributions over a discrete random variable $X$, and suppose the
    assumptions of Theorem~\ref{th:centrality} hold.
    Let $\tilde{P}$ be a nonnegative linear combination of elements of \setQ,
    and let $P$ be the corresponding normalized distribution.
    Then
    \[
        \myDiv[][Q] \leq \sup_{\inSet{Q}} \engFunc - \inf_{\inSet{Q}} \engFunc,
    \]
    where
    \[
        \engFunc = \entropy{Q}{X} + \Ex{Q}{\ln \tilde{P}(X)}.
    \]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    By Theorem~\ref{th:centrality}, $C$ is the unique central distribution of \setQ,
    and therefore from Equation~\ref{eq:cd-relation-to-lambda}, we have
    \[
        \myDiv[][Q] \leq \sFunc - \lambda.
    \]

    On the other hand, since $P$ is a convex combination of elements of \setQ,
    Lemma~\ref{lm:imperfection} implies that
    \[
        \inf_{\inSet{Q}} \kl \leq \lambda.
    \]

    Combining these two inequalities yields
    \begin{equation} \label{eq:cdiv-upper-bound}
        \myDiv[][Q] \leq \sFunc - \inf_{\inSet{Q}} \kl.
    \end{equation}

    Now let $\tilde{P}(X) = Z P(X)$, where $Z > 0$ is a normalization constant.
    Then, for any $Q$,
    \[
        \kl = \ln Z - \engFunc.
    \]
    Since $\ln Z$ does not depend on $Q$, the supremum and infimum translate directly into the corresponding
    expressions in terms of $\measure{F}{}{}$, and the result follows from Equation~\ref{eq:cdiv-upper-bound}.
\end{proof}

The upper bound in Equation~\ref{eq:cdiv-upper-bound} becomes small precisely when the variation of $\measure{F}{}{}$
across \inSet{Q} is small.
The theorem therefore suggests seeking a $\tilde{P}$ such that $\engFunc$ is approximately constant over \inSet{Q}.
This constant can be arbitrary, but it is convenient to choose it to be zero, in which case
\[
    \Ex{Q}{\ln \tilde{P}(X)} \approx - \entropy{Q}{X}.
\]

Note that the bound in Theorem~\ref{th:central-approx} is guaranteed only when $\tilde{P}$ is a non-negative
linear combination of elements of \setQ.
Furthermore, there must exist $\tilde{C}$, a strictly positive linear combination of elements of \setQ, such that
for every \inSet{Q} the equality holds:
\[
    \Ex{Q}{\ln \tilde{C}(X)} = - \entropy{Q}{X}.
\]
